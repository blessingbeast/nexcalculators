What is the Median?

The median is the middle value in a sorted dataset. It divides numbers into two equal halves — 50% of the values are below it, and 50% are above. Unlike the mean (average), the median is resistant to extreme values (outliers), making it a more accurate representation of "typical" in skewed datasets like incomes, property prices, and healthcare data.

Formula

If odd count: Median = Middle value after sorting

If even count: Median = Average of two middle values

Example Calculation

  • Odd dataset: {3, 7, 1, 9, 4} → Sorted: {1, 3, 4, 7, 9} → Median = 4
  • Even dataset: {8, 2, 6, 4} → Sorted: {2, 4, 6, 8} → Median = (4+6)/2 = 5

Real Life Use Cases

  • Income Statistics: India's median household income is more meaningful than average (which is skewed by ultra-high earners).
  • Property Prices: Median flat price in a locality gives a more realistic picture than average.
  • Exam Scores: Determine the typical performance in a class without one student's 0 or 100 distorting the picture.
  • Quality Control: Identify the central tendency of product defect rates.

FAQs

When should I use the median vs mean?

Use median when data has outliers or is skewed (income, property, age). Use mean when data is normally distributed and symmetric.

Can a dataset have multiple medians?

No. Every dataset has exactly one median value (though for even-count datasets, it's an average of the two middle values, which may not appear in the original data).
